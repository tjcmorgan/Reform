Reform

If you’re thinking about voting for Reform UK at the next election, it’s worth taking a step back and looking at the evidence.
 
It’s completely understandable why people feel frustrated. Living standards have been squeezed, public services are under pressure, and many voters feel that successive governments haven’t delivered meaningful improvements. When a party comes along promising to fix things quickly, that’s naturally appealing.
 
But we’ve heard that before.
 
In the run-up to Brexit, voters were told that leaving the European Union would free up £350 million a week for the NHS. That claim didn’t stand up then, and it certainly doesn’t now. What we have instead is more red tape, weaker trade flows, and slower economic growth—points reflected in analysis from the Office for Budget Responsibility.
 
And we’ve lost things that mattered in everyday life.
 
Before Brexit, UK citizens had the right to live and work freely across the EU. That meant the ability to take a job, build a career, or simply spend time in any of 27 countries without restriction. That freedom—especially valuable to younger people—has gone.
 
At the same time, the UK lost participation in the Dublin system, which allowed asylum seekers to be returned to the first EU country they entered. That made managing illegal migration more complex, not less.
 
Travel, too, is no longer as simple. British travellers now face separate queues at EU borders, increased checks, and delays that in busy periods can run into hours. A new travel authorisation system (ETIAS) is also being introduced, adding another layer of bureaucracy.
 
These are not abstract arguments. They are real-world consequences.
 
The argument now being made by Reform is, in many ways, a continuation of the same thinking: that the UK’s problems are primarily caused by immigration and external constraints, and that stepping further away from cooperation will fix them.
 
But the facts don’t support that.
 
Key sectors—especially the NHS—depend heavily on international recruitment. Restricting that supply without replacing it domestically creates shortages, not solutions. And many of the UK’s underlying problems—low productivity, regional inequality, chronic underinvestment—were here long before Brexit.
 
Before leaving, the UK held a uniquely strong position within Europe. Under David Cameron, we kept the pound, stayed out of the euro, and had veto power on major decisions. We were influencing outcomes, not reacting to them.
 
That influence is now gone.
 
And this is where voters need to pay closer attention.
 
Nigel Farage has argued that the West “provoked” Russia’s invasion of Ukraine through NATO and EU expansion. He has also previously said that the world leader he most admired was Vladimir Putin “as an operator.” You don’t have to agree with that to recognise what it signals: a tendency to frame Western alliances as the problem rather than authoritarian aggression.
 
More seriously, this isn’t just about rhetoric.
 
Nathan Gill, a former senior Reform figure in Wales, was jailed for over 10 years after admitting he took money to promote pro-Russian positions. Reform has also repeatedly had to drop or replace candidates over racist or offensive comments, including large numbers removed during the 2024 election cycle. That pattern matters—not because every individual is the same, but because it raises legitimate questions about judgement, standards, and leadership.
 
At the same time, Farage has aligned himself closely with Donald Trump.
 
That matters more than it used to.
 
If the United States becomes less predictable—as recent debates around NATO suggest—then Britain’s long-term security cannot rely on America in the same way it has for decades. In that world, the logical response is closer alignment with Europe, not further separation.
 
That’s not ideology. That’s strategy.
 
And there is another hard reality that often gets ignored.
 
Much of the argument against climate policy is that it is “too expensive.” But the economic risks of continued dependence on fossil fuels are already visible. Disruptions such as a closure of the Strait of Hormuz—through which a significant share of the world’s oil flows—can trigger sharp rises in energy prices, inflation, and the cost of living across the UK.
 
Those shocks are not theoretical—they are immediate and deeply damaging. In many scenarios, the cost of instability in global energy markets is comparable to, or greater than, the cost of transitioning to a more stable, lower-carbon economy.
 
And then there is the question of the future.
 
A February 2026 Savanta poll found that 83% of 16–24 year-olds would vote to rejoin the European Union, while only 17% would vote to stay out.
 
Put bluntly, among the younger generation, staying out of Europe is a minority position.
 
That matters.
 
Because younger people will live with the consequences of these decisions for far longer than those who made them.
 
The Brexit referendum itself was decided by a narrow margin—52% to 48%—on a question with long-term constitutional and economic consequences. It did not require a supermajority, and it did not distinguish between generations whose stakes in the outcome were vastly different.
 
You do not have to relitigate the past to see the point:
 
The future of this country belongs to those who will live in it the longest.
 
And increasingly, they are telling us they want a different relationship with Europe.
 
For what it’s worth, I have voted Liberal Democrat all my life, and I currently serve as a Deputy Mayor.
 
But this isn’t about party loyalty.
 
I have close friends across Conservative, Labour, and Green politics. And while I may disagree with them, I would argue that all of those parties offer at least a serious attempt at governing.
 
I also recognise and respect the work that a number of former Conservative councillors have contributed locally. That is why it is particularly disappointing to see some of them move towards Reform—a decision which I believe is misguided and unlikely to stand the test of time.
 
Even the Labour Party—which is already signalling a move toward closer integration with Europe—reflects a recognition of reality: our economic, social, and security interests are tied to our European neighbours.
 
Reform does not.
 
Its rhetoric is strong. But rhetoric is not policy. And when you look beyond the slogans, what you find is not a credible plan—but a politics that risks taking us further away from the cooperation we need.
 
If you are a committed Brexiteer, you may well dismiss all of this.
 
But it is worth asking whether that position is grounded in current reality—or in a story about Britain that no longer fits the world as it is.
 
We do need solutions to integration and cohesion in our communities. There is no question about that.
 
But division is not a solution.
 
And that is what Reform offers above all else.
